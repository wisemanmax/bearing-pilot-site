Resources

Six short reads on thinking clearly about the money — how it moves, where it hides, and what actually decides your options. No downloads, no gates — just the reasoning we use, written plainly so you can use it without us. If you want it applied to your own numbers, the free First Look is the place to start.

  1. 01° Read

    Reading the numbers that matter

    Most businesses track too many numbers and read too few. A dashboard full of metrics feels like control, but if none of them would change what you do next week, they are decoration.

    Start with a simpler test: which numbers, if they moved sharply, would force you to act? For most small businesses that shortlist is cash on hand, the gap between when money is earned and when it arrives, what it actually costs to deliver your main offer, and where new work comes from. Everything else is context.

    Then read those few numbers as directions, not snapshots. A single month's figure tells you almost nothing; the same figure moving the same way for three months is a message. Compare against your own recent past, not against averages for your industry — you run your business, not the average one.

    Finally, be suspicious of numbers that are always fine. A metric that never surprises you is usually measuring the wrong thing, or being read too generously. The useful numbers are the ones you would rather not look at.

  2. 02° Read

    Identifying the next constraint

    At any moment, one thing limits your growth more than everything else. It might be leads, capacity to deliver, cash to bridge the gap between the two, or your own attention. Working hard on anything other than that one thing produces motion without progress.

    To find it, ask: if demand doubled tomorrow, what would break first? If the honest answer is “we couldn't deliver it,” your constraint is capacity, and more marketing would make things worse, not better. If the answer is “we could handle it easily,” your constraint is demand, and process improvements can wait.

    Two cautions. First, the constraint moves — solving it reveals the next one, so this is a question to revisit each quarter, not settle once. Second, the constraint is often uncomfortable to name, because it is frequently the owner: a business where every decision routes through one person is constrained by that person's hours, whatever the marketing budget says.

    The discipline is to name one constraint, in writing, and let it decide where the next quarter's effort goes. If everything is a priority, you haven't found it yet.

  3. 03° Read

    Deciding when not to hire

    Hiring feels like progress, which is exactly why it deserves suspicion. A new salary is one of the largest recurring commitments a small business makes, and it is far easier to add than to unwind.

    Don't hire to fix a process problem. If work is chaotic, a new person inherits the chaos and adds coordination cost on top. Fix the process first, then ask whether the role still exists.

    Don't hire for peak load. If the overwhelm comes in bursts with slack in between, contractors, referrals to trusted peers, or simply saying no to marginal work all cost less than a permanent role sized for your busiest week.

    Don't hire to delegate a job you can't describe. If you cannot write down what the role does, what good performance looks like, and which tasks leave your plate on day one, you are hiring for relief, not for a role — and relief hires disappoint both sides.

    The hire is right when the work is well-defined, recurring rather than spiky, already crowding out higher-value work you should be doing, and affordable even if revenue stays flat. If those four don't all hold, the honest answer is not yet.

  4. 04° Read

    Choosing a useful 90-day measure

    A quarter is long enough for real change and short enough to stay honest. But a 90-day goal is only useful if you can tell, without debate, whether you hit it. Most fail on that point before the quarter starts.

    A useful measure has four properties. It is a number, not a feeling — “improve cash flow” invites argument; “shorten the average time from invoice to payment” does not. It has a baseline — if you don't know today's figure, your first week's work is measuring it, not moving it. It is something your actions actually reach in 90 days — revenue often moves too slowly and depends on too much outside you; the behaviors upstream of revenue move faster. And it is singular — three measures for one quarter means none of them decides anything.

    Pick the measure after you've named your constraint, not before. The right 90-day number is the one that tells you whether the constraint is loosening. Then write down the baseline, the target, and the date you'll check — and resist the urge to redefine the measure mid-quarter when it starts telling you something you don't like. That discomfort is the measure working.

  5. 05° Read

    The money you can actually decide with

    Two nonprofits can hold the same cash in the bank and be in completely different shape. The difference is how much of it they are allowed to spend on what's in front of them. Restricted funds — grants tied to a program, gifts with donor conditions — aren't yours to redeploy, even in a crunch. Unrestricted cash is. Confuse the two and you can feel flush while being one payroll away from trouble.

    The fix isn't complicated, but it has to be deliberate. Track restricted and unrestricted balances separately, in real time — not once a year at audit. Know, this week, how many days of unrestricted cash you have; that is the number that tells you what you can actually decide. And when a restricted grant lands, resist treating it as breathing room. It's already spoken for.

    Small businesses have a quieter version of the same problem: cash that's already committed — payroll, taxes owed, a supplier you can't stiff — versus cash that's genuinely free. The discipline is identical. The money you can decide with is never the balance in the account; it's what's left after everything already claimed against it. Know that number, and most cash panics simply don't happen.

  6. 06° Read

    What a bank actually underwrites

    When you ask a bank for a loan or a line of credit, they aren't really lending against your idea or your mission. They're lending against a small set of things they can verify — and if you know what those are, you stop guessing about what they'll say.

    They look at cash flow: not last year's, but whether the pattern of money in and out comfortably covers the new payment, with room to spare. They look at how clean your financials are — a lender who has to untangle your books prices that work into worse terms, or walks away. They look at concentration, because one customer or funder that is a third of your income is the risk that could take your repayment with it. And they look at whether you can explain your own numbers in the room, because a borrower who understands their financials is a safer bet than one who doesn't.

    None of that requires being bigger or more profitable than you are. It requires being legible. Before you need capital, get your last two years of financials clean, know your key ratios, and be able to walk a banker through them without flinching. The terms you're offered mostly reflect how much work it takes them to trust the numbers — and that's the part you control.
